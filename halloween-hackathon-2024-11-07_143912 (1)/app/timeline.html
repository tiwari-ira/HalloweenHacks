Timline

2000 BC: Origins of Samhain

Ancient Celts celebrate Samhain, marking the end of the harvest and the beginning of winter. This tradition forms the roots of modern Halloween.

8th Century: All Saints' Day

Pope Gregory III designates November 1 as All Saints' Day, incorporating some Samhain traditions. The evening before becomes known as All Hallows' Eve.

19th Century: Halloween Arrives in America

Irish immigrants bring Halloween customs, like carving pumpkins, to North America during the 1800s, helping Halloween gain popularity.

1920s-1930s: Trick-or-Treating Begins

The practice of trick-or-treating spreads in North America, with children dressing up and going door-to-door for candy.

1950s: Halloween Becomes a Family Holiday

Halloween grows as a family-friendly holiday in the U.S., with decorations, costumes, and candy becoming central traditions.

1978: Halloween Movie Released

1990s: Halloween Becomes Global

Halloween celebrations spread worldwide, with countries like the UK, Japan, and Australia adopting American traditions.

Present Day: Halloween's Popularity Grows

Halloween is now a multi-billion dollar industry in the U.S., celebrated with costumes, decorations, haunted houses, and parties.
