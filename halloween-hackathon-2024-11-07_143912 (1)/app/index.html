The Origins of Halloween

Halloween traces its roots back over 2,000 years to the ancient Celtic festival of Samhain, celebrated in Ireland, the United Kingdom, and northern France. During Samhain, the Celts believed that on October 31, the boundary between the living and the dead blurred, allowing spirits to wander among the living. To protect themselves, people lit large bonfires and wore costumes to ward off malevolent spirits. When Christianity spread, Samhain eventually merged with All Saints’ Day, forming a blend of pagan and Christian customs that evolved into Halloween as we know it today.

Why We Celebrate Halloween Today

Modern Halloween has become a lively celebration focused on fun, community, and imagination. Popular in many countries—especially the U.S., Canada, and parts of Europe—Halloween has become an occasion for families and friends to gather, decorate, and enjoy seasonal activities. Carving pumpkins, telling ghost stories, and going trick-or-treating are all ways people take part in the festivities. Children dress up in costumes and go door-to-door, receiving candy from neighbors in what has become one of Halloween’s most cherished traditions.

The Tradition of Costumes

The idea of wearing costumes dates back to the Celts, who wore animal skins and masks during Samhain to avoid being recognized by spirits roaming the Earth. Over time, this tradition transformed into an opportunity for creative expression, as people began to dress as mythical figures and popular characters. By the 20th century, costumes became widely available and central to the holiday, encouraging people of all ages to embrace Halloween’s playful and imaginative spirit. Today, costumes allow everyone to step into another world, making Halloween a celebration of creativity and transformation.
